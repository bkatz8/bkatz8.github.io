Awear

A mobile, non stigmatizing solution for PTSD management.

￼PTSD, or Post Traumatic Stress Disorder, affects around 5.2 million adults in the U.S. every year, making everyday life very difficult to cope with.

Design Prompt + Helping Out a Friend

This interactive products school project was limited by one design criteria: create an interactive product that solves a social issue and uses Arduino technology to do it. I worked on this project with college Grace Cha, who's awesome portfolio you can check out here!

￼For our project we decided to focus on the social issue of PTSD, since a close friend's father US Army Major Jeffrey Parks Sr. had just returned from duty in Afghanistan and knew several service men and women dealing with PTSD.

Issues with PTSD

  • PTSD can be triggered at random due to many stimuli and can cause depression, anxiety, nausea, and difficulty breathing.

  • Current coping methods for PTSD such as cognitive therapy and prescription drugs require constant help from others.

  • PTSD often leads to issues either for both individuals and families.

Design Objectives

Simple

If somebody's in a stressful situation, they shouldn't have to think about another thing in their way. The final design needs to be simple and to-the-point.

Non-stigmatizing

Many people going through PTSD, especially military vets, don't want to be stigmatized. Everyday, normal-looking is the key for this design project.

Mobile

Current help for PTSD such as medication and therapy are either not available on the go or require refilling a prescription. Design for mobile use is an essential key.

PTSD Research

Following some initial research, breathing exercises were found to be the most accepted and common form of stress relief used by PTSD patients. Moving forward, this would be the main centerpiece of the design.

Form Concepting

The most non stigmatizing wearable, the watch, was selecting as a starting point to begin sketching out some initial form ideas.

Tech Applications

To help PTSD as much as possible a range of sensors and tech options were looked at. The following were picked to add to the design. The reasoning for this is the ability of these sensors to aid in the breathing exercises intended to aid PTSD patients.

Galvanic Skin Response

Similar to what's used in lie detectors, GSR measures the electrical conductance of the skin. This depends on the body's stress levels and the skin's sweat.

Deep Pressure Stimulation

Already used in vests for calming patients with autism and other disorders, deep pressure stimulation through a watch is feels like a holding hand.

Capacitive Sensing

Capacitive sensing, often used in computer track pads, can be changed to read human presence with varying sensitivity. This makes it very versatile in various applications.

Final Form

A simple circular form was selected moving forward. The galvanic skin response and deep pressure stimulation unit were to be integrated into the wrist band for better readings. The capacitive sensor would go into the watch face for readings finger touches.

Coding + Prototyping

After the form was finished, the sensors and displays have to coding and integrated into the design. To visualize what the final design would do, a short storyboard was created.

When a user experiences stress, the watch would subtlety notify them. To start a set of breathing exercises, the user must hold their figure to the capacitive touch sensor. The breathing exercises would be complimented by a light cycle that encouraged proper breathing techniques at specific intervals. When stress levels returned to normal, the call to action would cease.

Pseudo code was written next to further understand what the sensors and displays needed to accomplish and when.

First round of Arduino code. Code was done in unison with an Arduino Flora, a AdaFruit neopixel ring, and an accelerometer in place of a GSR sensor and capacitive touch sensor for an initial prototype.

Awear + G-Shock

One exciting possible application for Awear is an internal integration into a military issued watch such as a G-Shock. Extremely durable and completely inconspicuous as a PTSD help tool, the G-Shock watch would be a perfect candidate for further development and integration of Awear.

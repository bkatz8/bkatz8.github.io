A multi-device solution for waitstaff feedback and management.

Introducing Google Waitstaff Management

Google Waitstaff is multi-device platform for taking waitstaff suggestions and using sound data metrics to improve and secure new employment.

Design Prompt

While there are many ways to rate and review restaurants, these are not focused on evaluating individuals servers. Design an experience where diners can submit positive comments and constructive suggestions for the wait staff, and servers can use this feedback to both improve and help to secure new employment.

Breaking Down the Prompt

Before I got anywhere near a pen to start designing or ideating, I took time to break down the prompt into components. I wanted to understand my design objectives and to begin listing the assumptions I carried with me going into the design exercise. I also jotted down notes to keep myself on track during the whole process so that I didn't stray too far off course. Keeping my ideas and research within the projects scale while considering the potential scale of Google were especially important.

Hypotheses + Assumptions

I took some time to get all of the information I believed to be true about the prompt and the issue at hand before moving forward. These were the things I initially assumed about the users, their behaviors, and their needs. Again, this process was all about rapidly getting a grasp on the scope of the problem and finding where I needed to get more information before moving forward.

Research + Interviews

After getting a general sense of where I wanted to take my design exercise and identifying required research, I decided to dive deeper into the scenarios surrounding the three user groups I indentified while breaking down the design prompt. These user groups were the following- customers/diners, waitstaff, and restaurant management.

The first step of this process was interviewing user groups to uncover current issues and frustrations, as well as areas of potential opportunity. You can view the full set of interview answers here! The results of the interviews uncovered the following key points that narrowed the focus of rest of the exercise:

1

Waitstaff performance and the resulting restaurant experience a product of management performance.

2

There is minimal waitstaff feedback from customers and management. Current feedback is very general and is mostly attributed to the kitchen quality.

3

The main metrics for measuring waitstaff performance are tips, friendliness, knowledgeability/awareness, and promptness.

I went to a local restaurant to get an idea of the time customers took during each phase of their meal to identify any possible times for constructive feedback to be given to waitstaff. The trip also uncovered how relationships between waitstaff and their customers.

Identifying Areas of Design Opportunity

After gathering a substantial amount of data and market research, I went back to the white board to identify the exact areas of oppotunity that would lend themselves to a memorable and useful design. The areas surrounding the end of the meal during payment, post-meal away from the restaurant, and late in the meal seemed the most promising based on my research, observations, and reflections on the design objectives. This rationale came especially from the in-depth task analysis I did of the the tradtional dining experience.

Finalized Design Objective

With all of my research, initial ideation, user personas and a dissected design prompt, I decided to create a revised design objective. The rationale behind the narrowing of the design prompt was to ensure that the design adressed the needs of all three user groups without distrupting the current flow of the dining experience.

Design an unobtrusive experience for constructive waitstaff feedback, criticism, and management, centered around the post-meal timeline.

Sketching + Ideation

With the refined design objective in mind, I started a speed ideation round to add upon all the information and design work I'd done already.

Non-Touch Interactions for Waitstaff Management

One of the topics I ideated upon that showed particular promise was the idea of using non-touch interactions to solve the issue of waitstaff feedback and management.
The traditional waiter booklet is a physical object that both waitstaff and restaurant-goers have familiarity with. The rationale behind this is adding a digital component to sign and review meal checks would gently shift users towards new experiences.

Ideation Refinement + Flows & Wireframes

Low-fidelity flows gave me starting point that I could base the rest of my flows and refined wireframes on. This is were I began to refine all of my ideas into a though-out design. The essence of this flow shows a two-device flow to most efficiently satisfy my design objective.

High-Level Flows and Wireframes

Below is a higher-level flow for my proposed solution. When a bill is given to a customer with the digital check booklet, they review their check. When they confirm the initial bill, users are prompted to provide feedback about the waitstaff using an extremely short, three-question survey based on the three metrics identified in my research in exchange for an incentive such as a meal discount or survey.

The data is then sent to cloud storage and shown in the form of data visualization on the management tablet experience. There managers and waitstaff can analyze individual team members' metrics, view overall waitstaff progress, manage incentives, manage checks, and take notes about waitstaff performace.

A high-level flow for the mobile check experience. The flow accounts for mutiple use cases (one check and mutiple checks) and also uses the industry standard of clock-wise service to ensure smooth bill payment.

More detailed high-level flows for the mobile check experience. Specifics of the information architecture used in the digital check was also addressed.

Management tablet experience high-level flows. The tablet experience shows visualized metrics for waitstaff tips, profit, friendliness, knowledgeability, and promptness addressed in my research.
